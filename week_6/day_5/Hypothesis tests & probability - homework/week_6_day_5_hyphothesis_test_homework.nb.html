The mean average_sleep for our imaginary Jabberwockies is 7. Now, we have taken this current sample of animals, and we want to know if the mean sleep_total for Jabberwockies differs significantly from the meean sleep_total of our animals data set?

First and most important task is to frame this question in terms of two competing hypotheses:

H0- the current mean Jabberwockies is the same as the mean sleep_total.

Ha- the current mean Jabberwockies is different as the mean sleep_total

We can write our two hypotheses more mathematically as:

H0: sleep_total = 7 Ha: sleep_total 7

Step 1 :let is first create an observed stat for our reference later

observed_stat <- msleep %>%
  summarise(average_sleep = mean(sleep_total))

observed_stat

So the mean(average_sleep) is slightly higher now than mean average_sleep for our imaginary Jabberwockies, but is the difference big enough for this to be statistically significant? That is, could this difference just be down to noise, or is it due to a real effect? A hypothesis test is the way to answer this!

First let is now set up our alpha = 0.05

Next, we treat the situation specified by the null hypothesis as if it were true, and use this to generate a sampling distribution, which we call the null sampling distribution. Well bootstrap to do this in the normal way

Calculating the null sampling distribution

null_distribution <- msleep %>%
  specify(response = sleep_total) %>%
  hypothesize(null = "point", mu = 7) %>%
  generate(reps = 10000, type = "bootstrap") %>%
  calculate(stat = "mean")

Now we can visualise the null distribution in the normal way

null_distribution %>%
  visualise(bins = 30)

Lets now plot this observed stats on the null distribution.

null_distribution %>%
  visualise(bins = 30) +
  shade_p_value(obs_stat = observed_stat$average_sleep, direction = "both")

Calculating p-value

p_value <- null_distribution %>%
  get_p_value(obs_stat = observed_stat$average_sleep, direction = "both")
p_value

Here the p-value is 0, and so we reject H0 in favour of Ha. The current mean average_sleep for our imaginary Jabberwockies is 7, which is significantly different to the value 10.43373 average_sleep for our data set of animals.

Q1. You work for a independent coffee shop. Youve performed a small survey in the local town and found that 40% of the people you randomly asked were aware of your shop in the town. You then conduct a marketing campaign by flyering local venues and targeting advertisements on social media. Finally you conduct a second small random survey in the town, asking if people are aware of your coffee shop. You want to test the hypothesis that the campaign has significantly increased awareness of the shop.

Ans : This is a case where in comparison is between two Paired (aka dependent) samples

We are interested in finding out: Is there a significant difference on percentage of people randomly surveyed who knew about the shop before the marketing campaing vs the percentage of people again randomly surveyed aware of the coffee shop after the marketing campaign?

Well stick with the conventional significance level =0.05 when answering this question.

One way we could address this is by adding a new column diff_new to the dataframe, which is the difference between the number of people who knew about the coffee shop before the campaign against the number of people who new about the coffee shop after the campaign.

H0 : diff_new=0 Ha: diff_new0

Significance level: 0.05, calculated p-value: 0.07

The p-value is grater than =0.05, so we fail to reject H0 and conclude that, on average, the percentage of random people surveyed and knew about the coffee shop before the marketing campaign does not differ significantly in camparison the the percentage of people surveyed randomly and knew about the coffee shop after the marketing campaign. Hence we can conclude that the marketing campaign was not a great success.

Ans 2 .

This is a case where in comparison is between two Paired (aka dependent) samples

We are interested in finding out: Is there a significant difference on proportion of people who click on the banner in its current position vs the proportion of people who click on the banner in its new position ?

Well stick with the conventional significance level =0.05 when answering this question.

One way we could address this is by adding a new column diff_new to the dataframe, which is the difference between the proportion of people who click on the banner in its current position against the proportion of people who click on the banner in its new position.

H0 : diff_new=0 Ha: diff_new0

Significance level: 0.01, p-value: 0.006

The p-value is lesser than =0.05, so we reject H0 and conclude that, on average, the proportion of people who click on the banner in its normal position differ significantly in camparison the the proportion of people who click on the banner in its new position. Hence we can conclude that moving the banner to its new position will make more sense and will get higher number of clicks.
